History of Quechua-Spanish Contact

  • Incans conquered much of western South America from the 13th to 16th century
  • Quechua became lingua franca, in contact with many languages, among them Aymara
  • Inca elites spoke extinct Puquina language

Quechua = official langues of Incas. Incan aristocracy used Puquina, now extinct. Prolonged & intense contact of Quechua, Puquina, and Aymara from 13th to 16th century.

Spanish Conquest in Western South America

During colonial period, Quechua used as lingua franca. Of the Spanish, merchants, administrators, and clergymen learned Quechua. Of the Andeans, children of indigenous elite, clergymen, & escribanos (helpers) learned Spanish.

Walls constructed in early 17th century to separate Spaniards & indigenous – very restricted contact.

Modern Language Policies

Indigenous languages are being recognized across LATAM, but of course there’s still a lot of stigma. Despite the gov’t language policies to support them, there is still a growing shift from Quechua to Spanish, especially as Quechua speakers move to urban centers.

  • Frequent use of diminutive on nouns, adj, gerunds, numerals, adverbs, pronouns (modesty)(corriendito, lejitos, ellita)
  • Frequent use of titles with first names (Doctor Gustavo, Señor Pedro, Licenciado Juan)

Semantic and Discursive Features

Morphosyntax in Contact (Sánchez, 2004)

Functional Convergence Hypothesis (FCH)

  • The FCH proposes that languages tend to converge (that is, become more similar) when they share similar functional features, rather than “only” words or sounds
  • How do languages encode meaning through grammar? (tense, agreement, word order, definiteness, etc.)
  • Goal: do bilinguals keep grammars fully separate or not?

Functional Convergence Hypothesis (FCH)

  • Possible scenarios:
- Languages share the structures, and so reinforce each other (positive transfer)
- Languages have different structures, and so their systems are reshaped

Extra Slides

Contact-Induced Language Change Criteria (Thomason, 2019)

  1. The recipient language must be examined as a whole, not single features in isolation. Multiple related features in Language B must point to external influence.

  2. There must be a plausible source Language A that was in sustained and intimate contact with Language B.

  3. A and B must share several features across different subsystems (e.g., syntax, morphology, phonology), not just a single feature.

  4. The feature must be old in Language A (i.e., not an innovation in the source language).

  5. The feature must be innovative (new) in Language B (i.e., not present in B before contact).
